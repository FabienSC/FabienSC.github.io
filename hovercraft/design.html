I had to design my hovercraft to meet certain criteria:

  • It had to be lightweight (and preferably float on its own without power)
  • It had to be big and sturdy enough to attach things like a GoPro onto it
  • It had to be made out of waterproof materials

For these reasons I built my hovercraft out of the following materials: an old swimming board for the base, a raincoat for the skirt and plywood for the rear motor housing.

The swimming board needed to have some groves and holes cut into it in order to have the components fit in and be securely attached. Its flat surface makes it easy to attach things onto it. The special foam it is made of is also sturdy and very boyant. If the weight is properly distributed on it, It should be able to stay afloat even without power.

I used the large back section of a raincoat to make the hovercraft's inflatable skirt. It is just a large tube with an entry hole that allows air from the front fan to fill it with air and small holes inside that release air into the cavity under the hovercraft and lift it slightly off the ground letting it glide like a puck on an air hockey table.

Finally I built a motor housing that holds the 2 ducted fan motors in place at the back of the hovercraft and allows them to swivel around. 2 servos are embedded into the swimming board directly underneath each motor. They control each motor's orientation.

As for the electronics, I chose to use the Arduino Mega 2560 board as it is quite affordable, I have lots of experience with it and it is easy to program. for my design, I built an arduino shield that allows it to use the radio transmitter. The 3 brushless motors are all controlled by Electronic Speed Controllers (ESCs) which themselves are connected to the shield.
